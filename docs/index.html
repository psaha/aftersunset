Moonlighting with solar-power collectors

The night sky is the source of countless beautiful pictures, and there is no grander collection than the archive of Astronomy Picture of the Day. Over two decades, they have shown everything from planets and moons to star-clusters to galaxies to the largest scales of the observable Universe. One category of astronomy pictures, however, is curiously lacking: pictures of individual stars (other than the sun). The best one can find is this fuzzy image of of the star Betelgeuse. Why is that?

The reason is that no current telescope has the sharpness to resolve any star in the night sky. Telescopes make stars look brighter, but they still appear as point of light, or rather as the diffraction pattern of a point source through the optics of a telescope. Resolving such a diffraction pattern into a picture of a star requires a sufficiently large telescope aperture. For a star of size \(S\) at distance \(D\) seen in light of wavelength \(\lambda\), the minimum telescope aperture needed is about \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \] Betelgeuse has \(S/D\) of about \(3\times10^{-6}\) That is, it has an angular size of 0.3 microradians. (Recall that a degree is \(\pi/180\) radians. The astronomers' unit of arc-second is a degree/360 or 6.7 microradians. The bar in the picture of Betelgeuse marked `10 mas' stands for 10 milli-arc-seconds or about 30 nano-radians.) For visible light \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \approx 3\rm\,metres \] for Betelgeuse. Now ground-based telescopes have to contend with turbulence in the atmosphere, which prevents them reaching their diffraction limit. Only space telescopes achieve their diffraction limit. The Hubble Space Telescope is not wide enough to resolve Betelgeuse, but its successor, the James Webb Space Telescope will just about manage. And that's just for Betelgeuse, which has an exceptionally large angular size. For most stars, you'd need a 100 metre aperture just to resolve them. And to resolve interesting features on their surface, apertures of a kilometre of more would be needed. It seems hopeless.

Interference with partially-coherent light

We know from wave optics that light from two or more sources comes together, the amplitudes add. But in our ordinary experience, intensities add. But intensity is amplitude squared. How can they both add??

The answer to this conundrum is not difficult, but it tells us something very important about interference, which isn't in old-fashioned optics textbooks.

In this and the following animations, consider the amplitude and phase of light as a single complex number, which we may call the complex amplitude.
Here we have two monochromatic sources, whose complex amplitudes are represented by the two blue lines. The revolving lines show the rotating phase. The two complex amplitudes are added, and the white dot represents the resultant complex amplitude.
As we can see, the two sources have slightly different frequencies. As a result, the two sources sometimes reinforce each other and sometimes cancel eath other. That is, they interfere sometimes constructively and sometimes destructively. The total intensity (squared radial distance of the white dot) beats between zero and four times the individual intensities.

Here we have 20 light sources, with slightly different frequencies. This corresponds to narrow-band or partially-coherent light.
The white dot is, in effect, the endpoint of a 20-step random walk in the complex plane. It's typical length is \(\sqrt{20}\) but in the animation varies from about twice that to near zero.
