Moonlighting with solar-power collectors

The night sky is the source of countless beautiful pictures, and there is no grander collection than the archive of Astronomy Picture of the Day. Over two decades, they have shown everything from planets and moons to star-clusters to galaxies to the largest scales of the observable Universe. One category of astronomy pictures, however, is curiously lacking: pictures of individual stars (other than the sun). The best one can find is this fuzzy image of of the star Betelgeuse. Why is that?

The problem

The reason is that no current telescope has the sharpness to resolve any star in the night sky. Telescopes make stars look brighter, but they still appear as point of light, or rather as the diffraction pattern of a point source through the optics of a telescope. Resolving such a diffraction pattern into a picture of a star requires a sufficiently large telescope aperture. For a star of size \(S\) at distance \(D\) seen in light of wavelength \(\lambda\), the minimum telescope aperture needed is about \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \] Betelgeuse has \(S/D\) of about \(2.5\times10^{-6}\). That is, its angular size is about \(2.5\times10^{-6}\) radians. Astronomers like to measure small sizes in arc-seconds, which is 1/3600 of a degree. Since a degree is \(\pi/180\) radians, and arc-second is about 5 micro-radians. Thus, in astronomers' units, the angular size of Betelgeuse is about 0.05 arc-seconds. The bar in the picture of Betelgeuse marked `10 mas' stands for 10 milli-arc-seconds. Red light has a \(\lambda\) of about 0.8 micro-metres. Thus \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \approx 3\rm\,metres \] for Betelgeuse in red light. Ground-based telescopes have to contend with turbulence in the atmosphere, which prevents them reaching their diffraction limit. Only space telescopes achieve their diffraction limit. The Hubble Space Telescope is not wide enough to resolve Betelgeuse, but its successor, the James Webb Space Telescope will just about manage. And that's just for Betelgeuse, which has an exceptionally large angular size. For most stars, you'd need a 100 metre aperture just to resolve them. And to resolve interesting features on their surface, apertures of a kilometre or more would be needed. It seems hopeless.

Interferometers

Fortunately, the aperture of a telescope can have gaps in it. In fact, it can be mostly gaps. That is, a few telescope elements spread over an area (say) a kilometre across can achieve the same resolution as a telescope a kilometre wide. The light-gathering power will be much less, of course. But the resolving power will be the same, provided the optical paths between all the elements are maintained in phase. Such telescopes are known as interferometers, and indeed are related to Michelson interferometers in optics labs.

Interferometric telescopes are standard at radio wavelengths. The most ambitious is the Square Kilometre Array which is under development now. It will have a collecting area of about a square kilometre, but an effective stretching across Africa and to Australia. It will have spectacular resolving power for radio sources in the sky, but it won't see stars, because stars don't have much radio-frequency emission. Astronomical interferometers at optical wavelengths also exist, for example the Sydney University Stellar Interferometers (SUSI) but none has gone to kilometre baselines. Keeping the optical paths coherent over that kind of baseline is very difficult.

An idea

There is, however, another kind of interferometry, which side-steps the challenge of keeping the optical paths coherent. It has different names, intensity interferometry, the Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect or just HBT. It is interferometry with partially coherent light. It requires much more light than ordinary interferometers, but it does not require optical paths to be precise to a wavelength. They need to more accurate than the time-resolution for detecting photons. With current technology that means about a millimetre, but much worse can be tolerated.

Interference with partially-coherent light

We know from wave optics that light from two or more sources comes together, the amplitudes add. But in our ordinary experience, intensities add. But intensity is amplitude squared. How can they both add??

The answer to this conundrum is not difficult, but it tells us something very important about interference, which isn't in old-fashioned optics textbooks.

In this and the following animations, consider the amplitude and phase of light as a single complex number, which we may call the complex amplitude.
Here we have two monochromatic sources, whose complex amplitudes are represented by the two blue lines. The revolving lines show the rotating phase. The two complex amplitudes are added, and the white dot represents the resultant complex amplitude.
As we can see, the two sources have slightly different frequencies. As a result, the two sources sometimes reinforce each other and sometimes cancel eath other. That is, they interfere sometimes constructively and sometimes destructively. The total intensity (squared radial distance of the white dot) beats between zero and four times the individual intensities.

Here we have 20 light sources, with slightly different frequencies. This corresponds to narrow-band or partially-coherent light.
The white dot is, in effect, the endpoint of a 20-step random walk in the complex plane. It's typical length is \(\sqrt{20}\) but in the animation varies from about twice that to near zero.

Which solar-energy collectors are best to work with?

The conceptually simplest design has a parabolic mirror tracking the sun, with a closed-cycle heat engine (a Stirling engine) at the focus, attached to a dynamo. As well as home-made examples by DIY enthusiasts, there are industrial versions by companies like Ripasso Energy and United Sun Systems.

Odeillo has a fixed main mirror, with movable plane mirrors to track the sun and reflect it onto the main mirror. This type of design associates different parts of the main mirror with very different optical path lengths. This type of design is fine for a solar furnace, but no good for interferometry.

Stirling engines are not far from the imaginary engines we know from Carnot's cycle, and are a whole fascinating topic on their own. This explanatory video explains how they work. Stirling engines have a reputation for being clean, efficient and low-maintenance, but heavy. Heaviness may explain why motor transport has never adopted them, but is not an issue when generating electricity.
