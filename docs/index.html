Moonlighting with solar-power collectors

The night sky is the source of countless beautiful pictures, and there is no grander collection than the archive of Astronomy Picture of the Day. Over two decades, they have shown everything from planets and moons to star-clusters to galaxies to the largest scales of the observable Universe. One category of astronomy pictures, however, is curiously lacking: pictures of individual stars (other than the sun). The best one can find is this fuzzy image of of the star Betelgeuse. Why is that?

The problem

The reason is that no current telescope has the sharpness to resolve any star in the night sky. Telescopes make stars look brighter, but they still appear as point of light, or rather as the diffraction pattern of a point source through the optics of a telescope. Resolving such a diffraction pattern into a picture of a star requires a sufficiently large telescope aperture. For a star of size \(S\) at distance \(D\) seen in light of wavelength \(\lambda\), the minimum telescope aperture needed is about \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \] Betelgeuse has \(S/D\) of about \(2.5\times10^{-6}\). That is, its angular size is about \(2.5\times10^{-6}\) radians. Astronomers like to measure small sizes in arc-seconds, which is 1/3600 of a degree. Since a degree is \(\pi/180\) radians, and arc-second is about 5 micro-radians. Thus, in astronomers' units, the angular size of Betelgeuse is about 0.05 arc-seconds. The bar in the picture of Betelgeuse marked `10 mas' stands for 10 milli-arc-seconds. Red light has a \(\lambda\) of about 0.8 micro-metres. Thus \[ \frac{\lambda S}D \approx 3\rm\,metres \] for Betelgeuse in red light. Ground-based telescopes have to contend with turbulence in the atmosphere, which prevents them reaching their diffraction limit. Only space telescopes achieve their diffraction limit. The Hubble Space Telescope is not wide enough to resolve Betelgeuse, but its successor, the JWST will just about manage. And that's just for Betelgeuse, which has an exceptionally large angular size. For most stars, you'd need a 100 metre aperture just to resolve them. And to resolve interesting features on their surface, apertures of a kilometre or more would be needed. It seems hopeless.

Interferometers

Fortunately, the aperture of a telescope can have gaps in it. In fact, it can be mostly gaps. That is, a few telescope elements spread over an area (say) a kilometre across can achieve the same resolution as a telescope a kilometre wide. The light-gathering power will be much less, of course. But the resolving power will be the same, provided the optical paths between all the elements are maintained in phase. Such telescopes are known as interferometers, and indeed are related to Michelson interferometers in optics labs.

Interferometric telescopes are standard at radio wavelengths. The most ambitious is the Square Kilometre Array which is under development now. It will have a collecting area of about a square kilometre, but an effective stretching across Africa and to Australia. It will have spectacular resolving power for radio sources in the sky, but it won't see stars, because stars don't have much radio-frequency emission. Astronomical interferometers at optical wavelengths also exist, for example the Sydney University Stellar Interferometers (SUSI). A similar instrument (the now-decommissioned IOTA) obtained the image of Betelgeuse linked above. None of these has gone to kilometre baselines. Keeping the optical paths coherent over that kind of baseline is very difficult.

There is, however, another kind of interferometry, which side-steps the challenge of keeping the optical paths coherent. It has different names, intensity interferometry, the Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect or just HBT. It is interferometry with partially coherent light. It requires much more light than ordinary interferometers, but it does not require optical paths to be precise to a wavelength. They need to more accurate than the time-resolution for detecting photons. The fastest photon counters nowadays have response times of maybe 10 picoseconds. So optical paths good to a millimetre are good enough. Less precise is not fatal, but it does degrade the signal, so more observation time is needed to build up the required signal-to-noise.

A stellar interferometer using partially coherent light was built by Hanbury Brown and colleagues in the 1960s. In fact, it pre-dates the SUSI. This great instrument had two six-metre mirrors movable over a railway track out to a separation of 120 metres. The photon-detector technology of the time was, however, primitive compare to today's. As a result, the instrument succeeded in measuring the sizes of about 30 stars, after which it ran out of stars bright enough for it to study, and was dismantled. There is interest reviving the idea with modern photon detectors and going to kilometre baselines. But there is no specific plan so far.

An idea

Collecting light and bringing it to a focus, without trying for optical coherence, has of course another and better-known application. The Odeillo solar furnace has been doing it for half a century. So do many other installations, which focus solar power to run a heat engine to generate electricity. And they are all just lying around at night. Could solar collectors be repurposed to usefully gather starlight for astronomy after sunset? Could solar-power collectors moonlight as interferometers?

This suggestion, due to Olaf Wucknitz, is just a crazy idea at the moment, and nobody is actively working in it. There is, however, a community of researchers working in the general area who will pay attention if the crazy idea becomes more realistic. The research questions below suggest some ways towards making the idea more realistic.

Development of such an interferometer would take years of work by many people, not to mention substantial investment. On the other hand, a credible design concept for an instrument to image the surfaces of stars far better than even the mighty JWST could do, and that while subsidising its own construction by putting electricity into the grid, should be pretty much unstoppable.

Interference with partially-coherent light

We know from wave optics that light from two or more sources comes together, the amplitudes add. But in our ordinary experience, intensities add. But intensity is amplitude squared. How can they both add??

The answer to this conundrum is not difficult, but it tells us something very important about interference, which isn't in old-fashioned optics textbooks.

In this and the following animations, consider the amplitude and phase of light as a single complex number, which we may call the complex amplitude.
Here we have two monochromatic sources, whose complex amplitudes are represented by the two blue lines. The revolving lines show the rotating phase. The two complex amplitudes are added, and the white dot represents the resultant complex amplitude.
As we can see, the two sources have slightly different frequencies. As a result, the two sources sometimes reinforce each other and sometimes cancel eath other. That is, they interfere sometimes constructively and sometimes destructively. The total intensity (squared radial distance of the white dot) beats between zero and four times the individual intensities.

Here we have 20 light sources, with slightly different frequencies. This corresponds to narrow-band or partially-coherent light.
The white dot is, in effect, the endpoint of a 20-step random walk in the complex plane. It's typical length is \(\sqrt{20}\) but in the animation varies from about twice that to near zero.

This animation shows a superposition of two partially coherent sources (blue and magenta), each of which itself consists of 20 coherent sources with slightly different frequencies.
Because of the random-walk-like addition of the complex amplitudes, the total amplitude is typically \(\sqrt2\) times as large as it would have been with just one of the `blue' or `magenta' sources. The intensity will be twice as large. This is why incoherent light is additive in intensities.

Below we have two animations. The left panel is actually the same as the previous animation. In the right panel, all the magenta amplitudes have been flipped in sign. Had all the frequencies been the same (that is, had the light been coherent) there would have been constructive interference on the left and destructive interference on the right. But what we have with partially coherent light is different. Both intensities vary, but they have a tendency to vary in an anti-correlated way. That is, flipping the sign of the magenta causes a statistical anti-correlation in the intensities. This is the Hanbury Brown and Twiss effect, and the basis of interferometry with partially coherent light.

Research questions

Which light sources are the most interesting?

The surface of the sun is pretty plain, but not so (according to theory) the surfaces of high-mass stars (including Betelgeuse). In high-mass stars, the internal radiation pressure is much higher than the gas pressure. Indeed, it is radiation that holds up these stars and prevents them from collapsing under their own gravity. Now, when you have a light fluid (in this case radiation) holding up a heavier fluid (hydrogen and helium gas) you get a turbulent boiling surface. The Betelgeuse image linked above hints at this, and would be very interesting to observe in detail.

Also important are close binary stars, where material passes from one to the other.

Still more exciting, if possible, would be to observe the environment of a massive black hole, which is lit up by gas heated up just before it falls in, with the event horizon itself forming a silhouette. The best candidate to try and resolve at optical wavelengths is the black hole at the centre of the galaxy Messier 87. There are ongoing observations of this system with interferometry at millimetre wavelengths with the eventual aim of imaging the shadow cast by the black hole, but they are not there yet.

Which photon counters should one use?

The basic observable in interferometry with partially coherent light is fluctuations in intensity. The more precisely one can measure the individual arrival times of photons, the better. A single-photon avalanche diode (SPAD) is nowadays the best type of device for this.

SPAD counters with very good time resolution are available from PicoQuant and Arrays of SPADs are still experimental but if and when they become usable they will provide a big improvement.

Which solar-energy collectors are best to work with?

Odeillo has a fixed main mirror, with movable plane mirrors to track the sun and reflect it onto the main mirror. This type of design associates different parts of the main mirror with very different optical path lengths. This type of design is fine for a solar furnace, but no good for interferometry.

The conceptually simplest design has a parabolic mirror tracking the sun, with a closed-cycle heat engine (a Stirling engine) at the focus, attached to a dynamo. As well as home-made examples by DIY enthusiasts, there are industrial versions by companies like Ripasso Energy and United Sun Systems.

Stirling engines resemble the imaginary engines we know from Carnot's cycle, and are a whole fascinating topic on their own. This explanatory video explains how they work. Stirling engines have a reputation for being clean, efficient and low-maintenance, but heavy. Heaviness may explain why motor transport has never adopted them, but is not an issue when generating electricity.

How about a prototype?

It would be interesting to repeat the classic experiment by Hanbury Brown and Twiss in 1958 measuring the angular size of Sirius using the same physical effect but modern instruments. Two amateur-class telescopes, equipped with high-end photon detectors and electronics, should be up to the task (detailed estimates here). In fact, even amateur-class telescopes would be rather overdoing it, since optical-quality mirrors are not necessary here. Adding small Stirling engines for daytime electricity generation would be a complete prototype.
